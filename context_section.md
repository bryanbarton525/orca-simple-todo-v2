## Context section\n\nRSS feeds have been the backbone of syndicating content on the web for decades, but their plain XML format is hard for most readers to digest. The data is machine‑centric, often lacking visual context or hierarchical grouping that aligns with how people read. Users must parse long lists of items, identify headlines, dates, and summaries manually, which leads to information overload. A newspaper‑style frontend transforms that raw XML into a familiar, structured layout that mirrors a physical newspaper: a headline, a brief lead, a thumbnail, and a clear hierarchy of stories. This visual metaphor reduces cognitive load, allowing readers to scan multiple stories at a glance and decide which to engage with. Moreover, the newspaper layout naturally groups content by sections or topics, providing contextual cues that are missing from a flat RSS list.\n\n```xml\n<rss version=\"2.0\">\n  <channel>\n    <title>Example Feed</title>\n    <item>\n      <title>Sample News Item</title>\n      <link>https://example.com/news/1</link>\n      <description>This is a sample description.</description>\n      <pubDate>Mon, 01 Jan 2021 12:00:00 GMT</pubDate>\n    </item>\n  </channel>\n</rss>\n```\n\nThe resulting HTML newspaper view renders the same data into a grid layout:\n\n```html\n<div class=\"news-article\">\n  <h2>Sample News Item</h2>\n  <p class=\"date\">Mon, 01 Jan 2021</p>\n  <p>This is a sample description.</p>\n  <a href=\"https://example.com/news/1\">Read more</a>\n</div>\n```
